The graphs collectively tell a story of evidence-based policy implementation that worked, but wasn't scaled sufficiently. Pre-treatment speed is the strongest predictor of post-treatment performance (≈97%). This underpins our threshold policy: prioritize ACE on corridors under 7–8 mph where uplift potential is greatest.

Multiple models perform reliably (R² ≥ 0.82; linear regression ≈0.85), enabling route ranking by predicted improvement. This supports a performance-based ACE expansion rather than ad hoc selection.

Clustering reveals 18.8% improvement potential for the slowest routes. This validates our focus on sub-8 mph corridors where ACE deployment shows consistent 15-19% gains.

Long-term trends show sustained improvements post-ACE implementation. Establish baseline monitoring before deployment to track meaningful changes over time.

Feature importance highlights express designation (~20%) and route complexity (~13%) as key signals for successful ACE candidates—favor longer, more complex routes when ranks are tied.

Manhattan shows lowest average speeds (7.1 mph) despite high density, making it priority for ACE expansion. Brooklyn and Bronx also below system average, while Staten Island already optimal.

Use predicted, bias-corrected uplift to allocate cameras: prioritize corridors with ≥ +0.5 mph expected gains; reallocate from >12 mph corridors that show diminishing or negative returns.
